CC theory
  1. Correlation effects
    1. Arises from "instantaneous" electron-electron interactions[1].
    2. This is in the post HF-methods (i.e to improve the HF-solution).
    3. The energetic correction to the HF-energy referred to the correlation energy.
  2. What is HF is missing?
    The HF is a mean field approximation, where we assume each electron moves in an average potential created by the other electrons. However, in reality,
    1. Electrons avoid each other dynamically. This is called the dynamical correlation.
    2. When electrons occupy near de-generate orbitals there is a static correlation.

[1] F. Neese, "Local Correlation Methods: From Theory to Implementation", ESQC Lecture (2022). https://www.esqc.org/lectures/ESQC22-Neese-Local-Correlation.pdf

Analogy about correlation
Imagine taking a photo of a crowded room where people avoid standing too close,
  1. HF photo: Shows people evenly spaced (average view), but some appear unnaturally close
  2. Correction (Correlation effect) photo: Add photos where some people step aside (excitations) to subtract the unrealistic crowding.
  3. Final (ground state or exact state) photo: The combination of these photos gives the true arrangement
Mathematically,
\[ E_{\text{exact}} = E_{\text{HF}} + E_{\text{Corr}} \]
Note that, $E_{\text{HF}}$ is always negative. It is an overestimation.

Why correlation is important?
  1. HF wave function
    In HF, we assume the wave function is well described by single slater determinant.
    \[ | \Psi_{\text{HF}} \rangle = | \phi_1 \phi_2 \cdots \phi_N \rangle \]
    where $\phi_i$ are the occupied molecular orbitals. This is single configuration of electrons in orbitals.
  2. Correlated wave function
    In reality, the wave function is of the form,
    \[ | \Psi_{\text{Corr}} \rangle = C_0 | \phi_1 \phi_2 \cdots \phi_N \rangle + C_1 | \phi'_1 \phi'_2 \cdots \phi'_N \rangle + \cdots \]
    where $\phi'_i$ is an alternative configuration. HF ignores these terms and cause errors. This is the reason why we need to take correlation into the account.

Coupled Cluster Theory
Coupled Cluster Ansatz
\[ | \Psi_{\text{CC}} \rangle = e^T | \Psi_0 \rangle \]
where the cluster operator \(T = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots\) and
\[ T_1 = \sum_{i = 1}^{N_{\text{occ}}} \sum_{a = 1}^{N_{\text{vir}}} t_i^a a_a^\dagger a_i \quad , \quad T_2 = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{i, j = 1}^{N_{\text{occ}}} \sum_{a, b = 1}^{N_{\text{vir}}} t_{ij}^{ab} a_a^\dagger a_b^\dagger a_j a_i \]
where $i,j,k,...$ represents occupied orbitals and $a,b,c,...$ virtual orbitals.
CC Energy Equation
\[ E_{\text{CC}} = \langle \Psi_0 |e^{-T} H e^T | \Psi_0 \rangle \]
Residual/amplitude Equations
\[ R_{\mu} = \langle \Psi_{\mu} | e^{-T} H e^T | \Psi_0 \rangle = 0 \]

Local Correlation
Idea: Both dynamic and static electron correlation effects are short range. Therefore, the consideration of localization is not a surprise.
The simple answer to the question "what is local correlation?" is decomposing the system (molecule) into localized regions and treating correlation dominantly within these regions and neglecting weak long range correlations.
For a example: atoms, atomic orbitals, bonds, fragments, etc. (Frank calls them as chunks)

    2. These orbitals are typically higher in energy than the occupied orbitals and can be accessed through excitations.

Localization in internal space
Canonical molecular orbitals (MOs) are eigenfunctions of the Fock space and are typically delocalized over the entire molecule. Localized orbitals, however, describe chemically intuitive features like,
  1. Core electrons (localized near nuclei).
  2. Lone pairs (localized on specific atoms).
  3. Bonds (localized between pairs of atoms).
Mathematical Framework is,
First, given a set of occupied canonical molecular orbitals (MOs) \(\{ \Psi_i \}_{i=1}^{N_{\text{occ}}}\), we seek a unitary transformation \(U\) that produces localized orbitals \(\{ \psi_i \}_{i=1}^{N_{\text{occ}}}\):
\[ \psi_i = \sum_{j=1}^N U_{ji} \Psi_j \]

Unitary transformation
The key idea is that a unitary transformation rotates the delocalized canonical orbitals into a new set of orbitals that are spatially localized, while preserving their orthonormality and total electron density. Here’s the intuition,
Norm preservation: \(\langle \psi_i | \psi_i \rangle = 1\) (orbitals stay normalized).
Orthogonality preservation: \(\langle \psi_i | \psi_j \rangle = 0\) (orbitals remain orthogonal).
Total electron density unchanged: \(\sum_i |\psi_i|^2 = \sum_i |\Psi_i|^2\).
The unitary transformation optimizes a localization criterion, effectively "focusing" each orbital into a smaller spatial region. Here’re some methods,
  1. Foster-Boys (FB) localization.
  2. Pipek-Mezey (PM) localization.

FB localization
Different localization methods optimize different criteria. Here we will discuss particulary about FB,
Key idea is we minimize the sum of orbital variances and this leads to localized spatial spread.
\[ \Omega = \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \langle \psi_i | r^2 | \psi_i \rangle - \langle \psi_i | \mathbf{r} | \psi_i \rangle^2 \right) \]
The spread ($\Omega_i$) of an orbital $\psi_i$ measures how much its electron density is "smeared out" in space. Mathematically, it's the variance of the position operator $\mathbf{r}$ for the orbital.
\[ \Omega_i = \langle \psi_i | r^2 | \psi_i \rangle - \langle \psi_i | \mathbf{r} | \psi_i \rangle^2 \]
Smaller $\Omega_i$ means the orbital is more localized (electron density concentrated near its center).
Foster-Boys aims to minimize the sum of spreads for all occupied orbitals,
\[ \Omega = \sum_{i=1}^N \Omega_i \]
This forces each orbital to, shrinks toward a point (like squeezing a balloon to reduce its volume) and avoid overlapping tails (delocalized "fringes" of orbitals are suppressed).

FB intuiation
  1. Chemical Interpretation
    1. Core electrons: Naturally localized near nuclei (tiny spread).
    2. Bonds: Concentrated between two atoms (small spread along the bond axis).
    3. Lone pairs: Localized on one atom (compact, like a "lump" of density).
    By minimizing $\Omega$, FB transforms delocalized MOs into these chemically intuitive objects.
  2. Analogy to Classical Physics
    3. Imagine each orbital as a probability cloud (like a blob of charge),
    1. Delocalized orbital: A diffuse cloud (high variance).
    2. Localized orbital: A tight, dense cloud (low variance).
    FB is like pulling the cloud’s edges inward to make it as compact as possible..

Localization procedure via (FB) localization
Goal,
Minimize the functional $\left(\Omega = \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \langle \psi_i | r^2 | \psi_i \rangle - \langle \psi_i | \mathbf{r} | \psi_i \rangle^2 \right)\right)$ This corresponds to minimizing the spread (variance) of each orbitals's position and key steps in minimization are,
  1. Start with the occupied MOs (typically start with canonical HF orbitals).
  2. Construct the Boys functional, for each pair of localized orbitals, evaluate the orbital centers.
    3. \[\mathbf{r}_i = \langle \psi_i | \mathbf{r} | \psi_i \rangle\]
  3. The algorithm performs pairwise Jacobi rotations among the orbitals.
    1. Choose a pair (i, j)
    2. Optimize a unitary rotation matrix to reduce the spread
      3. Here, rotation matrix is $U(\theta)$
    3. Rotate the orbitals
      4. $\psi_i{\prime}, \psi_j{\prime} = U(\theta) (\psi_i, \psi_j)$
    4. Repeat until convergence (Convergence Criteria: Stop when the change in the Boys functional is below a threshold (e.g., 10^{-8}))

Localization in virtual space - challenges
Frank and many other reputed chemists say "localization in the virtual space is challenging". Let's start our discussion with some challenges,
  1. Virtual orbitals are diffuse and lack a clear spatial center.
    2. Higher Energy & Weaker Nuclear Attraction.
    Nodal Structure & Lack of Electron Occupation.
  2. They are nearly degenerate in energy, making standard localization criteria less effective.
    3. Virtual orbitals often lie close in energy, making them prone to mixing under small perturbations.
    Unlike occupied orbitals (which are well-separated due to exchange interactions), virtual orbitals can easily hybridize, further increasing their delocalization.

Pair Natural Orbitals (PNOs)
Best way to understand "what are PNOs?" is learn the derivation. Here's a high-level idea how do get PNOs[5],
  1. For a large molecule, get the optimized HF-SCF MOs.
  2. Obtain MP2 guess for the cluster amplitudes using the canonical HF occupied and virtual orbitals.
  3. Define "pair density" for each pair of localized occupied orbitals (i,j). These pair densities are defined solely in terms of virtual orbitals.
  4. Diagonalize the pair density matrix. This gives the "pair-natural orbital" occupation numbers and the PNO coefficient vectors.
    5. (Note: The term "natural orbitals" in general is used for designating a set of orbitals that diagonalizes the one-particle density matrix, e.g., MCSCF natural orbitals. The name "pair-natural orbitals" also derives from the same concept. The PNOs diagonalize the pair density matrix for every pair of localized occupied orbitals.)

[5] "What are pair natural orbitals?", Matter Modeling Stack Exchange (2020). https://mattermodeling.stackexchange.com/questions/3953/what-are-pair-natural-orbitals

PNOs in depth - Part 1
Start with constructing the "Pair-Specific Density Matrix" (Assumption we already have MP2 cluster amplitudes for the virtual space),
For each occupied electron pair $(ij)$, we define the "pair density matrix" in the virtual orbital space.
\[ PD(\text{for pair}-ij) = \sum_{a,b =1}^{N_{\text{vir}}} t_{ij}^{ab} t_{ij}^{ba^*} \in \mathbb{C}^{N_{\text{vir}} \times N_{\text{vir}}} \]
where, MP2 amplitudes are, \( t_{ij}^{ab} = \dfrac{\langle ij \vert \vert ab \rangle}{\epsilon_i + \epsilon_j - \epsilon_a - \epsilon_b}\) for $a,b \in N_{\text{vir}}$
Next we diagonalize the "Pair Density Matrix",
We diagonalize $PD$ to obtain its eigenvalues (occupations) and eigenvectors (transformation matrix).
\[ PD = \sum_{k=1}^{N_{\text{vir}}} n_k V_k V^*_k \]
$n_k$ - occupation numbers (sorted in descending order)
$U = V V^*$ - unitary transformation matrix

PNOs in depth - Part 2
Continue,
Then we transform the canonical virtual MOs to PNOs using contructed unitary matrix (reduced),
\[ \chi^{ab}_k(ij) = U \Psi^{ab}_{ij} \quad\forall k \in N_{\text{vir}} \]
We understand that the eigen-values $n_k$ represents the contribution significace of the virtual orbitals to the choosen electron pair. Thus we take the advantage here by introducing a threshold (called $T_{\text{CutPair}}$) to select the most significant virtual orbitals contributions. This reduces the number of PNOs which we are gojng to contruct significantly without loosing accuracy.
i.e, we retain only PNOs with significant occupations
Keep $\chi^{ab}_k(ij)$ if $n_k > \tau \quad (\tau \approx 10^{-6})$.

LPNOs and DL-PNOs
LPNOs,
LPNOs (Local Pair Natural Orbitals) are an extension of PNOs that further improve computational efficiency by incorporating spatial locality into the electron pair correlation treatment.
If we carefully recall the steps to derrive the PNOs[5],
  1. Here, we use localize the occupied orbitals using Pipek-Mezey or the Foster-Boys scheme.
  2. And obtain MP2 guess for the cluster amplitudes using localized occupied orbitals and the canonical HF virtual orbitals.
Since we used the localized occupied orbitals the deriived PNOs are defined as "Local Pair Natural Orbitals" (LPNOs).
DL-PNOs,
DL-PNOs (Domain-Based Local Pair Natural Orbitals) are an extension of LPNOs that further improve computational efficiency by incorporating using PAOs as the domain of the virtual MOs instead of canonical virtual MOs[5].
Let PAOs defined as, $\xi_l l \in N'_{\text{vir}}$ then new unitary transormation is,
\[ \chi^{ab}_l(ij) = U \xi^{ab}_{ij} \quad\forall l \in N'_{\text{vir}} \]

Construction of the N-Particle Space
We can construct a complete N-electron expansion[1] set from the solutions of the HF equations by replacing 1,2,...,N electrons in the HF Slater-determinant by virtual orbitals (so called excitations - not to be confused with actual excited STATES)!
\[ \Psi = C_0 \Phi_{\text{HF}} + \underbrace{ \sum_{ia} C_i^a \Phi_i^a }_{\text{Singles}} + \underbrace{ \sum_{ijab} C_{ij}^{ab} \Phi_{ij}^{ab} }_{\text{Doubles}} + \underbrace{ \sum_{ijkabc} C_{ijk}^{abc} \Phi_{ijk}^{abc} }_{\text{Triples}} + \dots + \underbrace{ \vphantom{\sum_{ijkabc}} \text{exc.} }_{\text{(n-fold)}} \]
Conventions,
$i,j,k,l \rightarrow$ internal (occupied) orbitals (occupied in the HF determinant)
$a,b,c,d \rightarrow$ external (virtual) orbitals (unoccupied in the HF determinant)
$p,q,r,s \rightarrow$ general orbitals (occupied or unoccupied)

[1] F. Neese, "Local Correlation Methods: From Theory to Implementation", ESQC Lecture (2022). https://www.esqc.org/lectures/ESQC22-Neese-Local-Correlation.pdf

\[ \hat{H}_{BO}\left(C_0 \Phi_{HF} + \sum_{ia} C_i^a \Phi_i^a + \left(\frac{1}{2!}\right)^2 \sum_{ijab} C_{ij}^{ab} \Phi_{ij}^{ab} + \dots\right) = E\left(C_0 \Phi_{HF} + \sum_{ia} C_i^a \Phi_i^a + \left(\frac{1}{2!}\right)^2 \sum_{ijab} C_{ij}^{ab} \Phi_{ij}^{ab} + \dots\right) \]
Multiplying with the HF function from the left,
\[ C_0 \underbrace{ \left\langle \Phi_{HF} \middle| \hat{H}_{BO} \middle| \Phi_{HF} \right\rangle }_{E_{HF}} + \sum_{ia} C_i^a \underbrace{ \left\langle \Phi_{HF} \middle| \hat{H}_{BO} \middle| \Phi_i^a \right\rangle }_{F_{ia}} + \frac{1}{4} \sum_{ijab} C_{ij}^{ab} \underbrace{ \left\langle \Phi_{HF} \middle| \hat{H}_{BO} \middle| \Phi_{ij}^{ab} \right\rangle }_{\left\langle ij \middle\| ab \right\rangle} \] \[ = E (\text{Corr}) \left( C_0 \underbrace{ \left\langle \Phi_{HF} | \Phi_{HF} \right\rangle }_1 + \sum_{ia} C_i^a \underbrace{ \left\langle \Phi_{HF} | \Phi_i^a \right\rangle }_0 + \frac{1}{4} \sum_{ijab} C_{ij}^{ab} \underbrace{ \left\langle \Phi_{HF} | \Phi_{ij}^{ab} \right\rangle }_0 \right) \]

Nesbet's and Brillouin's Theorems - Part 2
Continue,
$F_{ia} = h_{}ia + \sum_j \langle ij || aj \rangle \rightarrow$ Matrix element of the HF operator
$\epsilon_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{ab} C_{ij}^{ab} \langle ij || aj \rangle \rightarrow$ Pair correlation energy
where, $\langle ij || ab \rangle = \langle ij | ab \rangle \langle ij | ba \rangle\rightarrow$ Anti-symmetrized ERIs
We get[1],
\[ \sum_{ia} C_a^i F_{ia} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{ij} \varepsilon_{ij} = C_0 E_{\text{corr}} \]
However, if the orbitals really do satisfy the canonical HF equations[1],
\[ F\psi_i = \varepsilon_i \psi_i \]

Nesbet's and Brillouin's Theorems - Part 3
Continue,
Then we see immediately by multiplying from the left with $\psi_a$ and integrating[1],
\[ \sum_{ia} C_a^i F_{ia} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{ij} \varepsilon_{ij} = C_0 E_{\text{corr}} \]
However, if the orbitals really do satisfy the canonical HF equations[1],
\[ \langle \psi_a \mid F \mid \psi_i \rangle = \varepsilon_i \langle\psi_a \mid \psi_i \rangle = 0 \]
Note: this does not imply that the coefficients on the single excitations in the exact wavefunction are zero! They are not zero for HF orbitals. (there is a set of orbitals for which the singles coefficients are zero, the so called Brueckner orbitals).
Thus, for HF orbitals the matrix elements of the BO Hamiltonian with single excited determinants is zero! (Brillouin‘s theorem). Finally[1],
\[ \frac{1}{4} \sum_{ijab} C_{ab}^{ij} \Braket{ij \| ab} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{ij} \varepsilon_{ij} = C_0 E_{corr} (\text{Nesbets's theorem}) \]
If we would know the precise values of the double excitation coefficients we would know the "EXACT" correlation energy!
