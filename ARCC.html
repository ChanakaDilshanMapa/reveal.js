CC theory - Intro
Coupled Cluster ansatz,
\[ \boxed{ | \Psi_{\text{CC}} \rangle = e^T | \Psi_0 \rangle } \]
where the cluster operator \(T = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots\) and
\[ T_1 = \sum_{i = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{occ}}} \sum_{a = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{vir}}} t_i^a a_a^\dagger a_i \quad , \quad T_2 = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{i, j = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{occ}}} \sum_{a, b = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{vir}}} t_{ij}^{ab} a_a^\dagger a_b^\dagger a_j a_i \quad, ... \]
where $i,j,k,...$ denotes occupied orbitals and $a,b,c,...$ virtual orbitals and $\mathcal{N}_{\text{occ}}, \mathcal{N}_{\text{vir}}$ denotes no. of occupied and virtual orbitals respectively. Here, $t$ denotes the CC amplitudes and $a^\dagger,a$ denotes creation and annihilation operators respectively.
\[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} E_{\text{CC}} &= \langle \Psi_0 | e^{-T} H e^T | \Psi_0 \rangle && \text{(CC energy equation)} \\[6pt] R_x &= \langle \Psi_x | e^{-T} H e^T | \Psi_0 \rangle = 0 && \text{(Residual / amplitude equations)} \end{aligned} } \]

CC theory in an arbitrary gauge
Idea: We use a unitary matrix for the basis transformation.
\[ \boxed{ \begin{array}{c} \text{CC theory} \\ \text{(canonical basis)} \end{array} } \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{Basis transformation } (U)\;}\; \boxed{ \begin{array}{c} \text{CC theory} \\ \text{(arbitrary basis)} \end{array} } \]
AR basis is not, in general, orthogonal. To ensure orthogonality, we define,
\[ \bar{U}_{\mu i} = S_{\mu}^{\alpha} U_{\alpha i}, \]
where "$\mu$" is an AO index and "$S$" denotes the overlap matrix in the AO basis which has the property,
\[ U_{\mu p} \bar{U}_{\mu q} = U_{\mu p} S_{\mu}^{\alpha} U_{\alpha q} = \delta_{pq}. \]

Projectors
The integral transformation procedure contains 2 steps:
\[ \boxed{ \begin{array}{c} \text{AO} \\ \text{Integrals} \end{array} } \;\xrightarrow{\;C\;}\; \boxed{ \begin{array}{c} \text{MO} \\ \text{Integrals} \end{array} } \;\xrightarrow{\;U\;}\; \boxed{ \begin{array}{c} \text{AR} \\ \text{Integrals} \end{array} } \]
where "$C$" denotes the molecular coefficient matrix. Also during the process, we must avoid mixing between occupied and virtual spaces, the explicit projection onto the individual spaces will be required.
The projectors onto the occupied and virtual subspace take the form[1],
\[ P_{\mu}^{\nu} = C_{\mu i} U_{\nu i}, \quad \quad \quad Q_{\mu}^{\nu} = U_{\mu a} C_{\nu a}. \]
together with their overlap-weighted counterparts [1],
\[ \bar{P}_{\mu}^{\nu} = \bar{U}_{\mu i} C_{\nu i}, \quad \quad \quad \bar{Q}_{\lambda}^{\sigma} = \bar{U}_{\lambda a} C_{\sigma a}. \]
with the following properties,
\[ PSP = P, \quad QSQ = Q, \quad PSQ = 0, \quad P+Q = S^{-1}. \]

[1] G. E. Scuseria and P. Y. Ayala, Linear scaling coupled cluster and perturbation theories in the atomic orbital basis, J. Chem. Phys. 111, 8330–8343 (1999).

Elements in AR basis
We now derive the transformed electron repulsion integrals,[1] as
\[ \pi_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} = P_{\mu\alpha} P_{\nu\beta} \nu_{\alpha\beta}^{\gamma\delta} Q_{\gamma\lambda} Q_{\delta\sigma}. \]
where "$\nu,\lambda,\sigma$" are also AO indices.
The corresponding transformed amplitudes[1] as,
\[ \theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} = \bar{U}_{\mu i} \bar{U}_{\nu j} \bar{U}_{\lambda a} \bar{U}_{\sigma b} t_{ij}^{ab}. \]
Note that the amplitudes in the MO basis obey the symmetry $t_{ij}^{ab} = t_{ji}^{ba}$, which is directly inherited by the transformed amplitudes in the AR representation, i.e., $\theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} = \theta_{\nu\mu}^{\sigma\lambda}$.
With these we can transform CC equation[1] from MO basis to AR basis as,
\[ Z_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} (\theta) = \bar{U}_{\mu i} \bar{U}_{\nu j} \bar{U}_{\lambda a} \bar{U}_{\sigma b} \, Z_{ij}^{ab} (t) = 0. \]

[1] G. E. Scuseria and P. Y. Ayala, Linear scaling coupled cluster and perturbation theories in the atomic orbital basis, J. Chem. Phys. 111, 8330–8343 (1999).

Correlation energy in AR basis
The correlation energy in the AR basis[1],
\[ E_{\text{corr}} = \Pi^{\mu\nu}_{\lambda\sigma} \, \theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} = \pi^{\mu\nu}_{\lambda\sigma} \, \Theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma}, \]
where,
\[ \begin{aligned} \Pi^{\mu\nu}_{\lambda\sigma} &= 2\pi^{\mu\nu}_{\lambda\sigma} - \pi^{\mu\nu}_{\sigma\lambda}, \\ \Theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} &= 2\theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} - \theta_{\mu\nu}^{\sigma\lambda}. \end{aligned} \]
Note: One can compute the correlation energy and check across bases as a sanity check as the correlation energy is gauge invariant.

[1] G. E. Scuseria and P. Y. Ayala, Linear scaling coupled cluster and perturbation theories in the atomic orbital basis, J. Chem. Phys. 111, 8330–8343 (1999).

Fixed point map transformation
In the MO basis, a widely adopted fixed-point map for the doubles amplitudes is given by[2],
\[ \boxed{ t^{ab}_{ij} = \frac{v^{ab}_{ij} + \Delta f^{ab}_{ij}(t) + \bar{R}^{ab}_{ij}(t)}{f^{i}_{i} + f^{j}_{j} - f^{a}_{a} - f^{b}_{b}} } \]
Replacing each tensor in the fixed point map with their transformed counterpart leads to[1],
\[ \boxed{ \theta^{\lambda\sigma}_{\mu\nu} = \frac{d^{\lambda\sigma}_{\mu\nu} + \Delta F^{\lambda\sigma}_{\mu\nu}(\theta) + R^{\lambda\sigma}_{\mu\nu}(\theta)}{{}^{o}F^{\mu}_\mu + {}^{o}F^{\nu}_\nu - {}^{v}F^{\lambda}_\lambda - {}^{v}F^{\sigma}_\sigma} } \]

[1] G. E. Scuseria and P. Y. Ayala, Linear scaling coupled cluster and perturbation theories in the atomic orbital basis, J. Chem. Phys. 111, 8330–8343 (1999).

[2] I. Shavitt and R. J. Bartlett, Many-Body Methods in Chemistry and Physics: MBPT and Coupled-Cluster Theory, Cambridge Molecular Science, Cambridge University Press (2009).

Orbital energies and unperturbed Hamiltonian
The electronic Hamiltonian in second quantization can be written as[2],
\[ H = H_0 + V, \]
where $H_0$ is the unperturbed Hamiltonian, and $V$ consists of the remaining terms as the perturbation.
In Møller-Plesset perturbation theory[2],
\[ H = F + V, \]
where $F$ is the many-body Fock-Operator (the diagonal structure of the Fock operator in the MO basis motivates the definition of the zeroth-order Hamiltonian) and define as,
\[ H_0 = F = \sum_{p,q = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{basis}}} F_{pq} a_p^\dagger a_q, \]
where $\mathcal{N}_{\text{basis}}$ is the no. of basis functions. $F_{pq}$ is the Fock matrix in MO basis.

[2] I. Shavitt and R. J. Bartlett, Many-Body Methods in Chemistry and Physics: MBPT and Coupled-Cluster Theory, Cambridge Molecular Science, Cambridge University Press (2009).

Orbital energies and unperturbed Hamiltonian
And $V$ is the fluctuation potential and define as,
\[ V = H - H_0 = H - F = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{p,q,r,s = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{basis}}} v_{pq}^{rs} \, a_p^\dagger a_q^\dagger a_s a_r - \sum_{p,q = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{basis}}} F_{pq} a_p^\dagger a_q, \]
where $a_p^\dagger$ and $a_q$ the usual creation and annihilation operators. Within coupled-cluster theory, the CCD doubles amplitudes are updated via the canonical fixed-point iteration.
The denominator,
\[ \Delta_{ij}^{ab} = \varepsilon_i + \varepsilon_j - \varepsilon_a - \varepsilon_b, \]
is the energy difference between the excited eigenstate $\lvert \Phi_{ij}^{ab} \rangle$ and the unperturbed ground state of $H_0$. In the MO basis, simplifies to,
\[ \boxed{ H_0 = \sum_{p = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{basis}}} \varepsilon_p \, a_p^\dagger a_p. } \]

Spectrum of the many-body Fock operator
And the eigenvectors of the many-body Fock operator are all possible Slater determinants constructed from the MOs,
\[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} V_{\mathcal{I}} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathcal{N}_{\text{e}}!}} \bigwedge_{i = 1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{e}}} \phi_{k_i} \, \forall \,\mathcal{I}, \\ \mathcal{I} &= \{ k_1 < k_2 < \cdots < k_{\mathcal{N}_{\text{e}}} \} \subset \{1, 2, \dots, \mathcal{N}_{\text{basis}} \}, \quad \mathcal{N}_{\text{e}} = |\mathcal{I}|. \end{aligned} } \]
where $\phi$'s are the MOs and $\mathcal{N}_{\text{e}}$ is the number of electron in the system. Given the corresponding eigenvalue is simply the sum of the orbital energies of the occupied MOs,
\[ \boxed{ \lambda_{\mathcal{I}} = \sum_{i=1}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{e}}} \varepsilon_{k_i} } \]
Thus, the energy denominators in the fixed-point map are directly related to the eigenvalues f $H_0$ through perturbation theory. Using the diagonal elements of the AO Fock matrix as approximate orbital energies breaks this correspondence.

MO Fock matrix and Unperturbed Hamiltonian
In the AO basis, the diagonal elements of the Fock matrix does not correspond to $H_0$. In particular, they may produce artificially small HOMO-LUMO gaps and $\Delta$ become sufficiently small and diverge, even for molecules whose true orbital gaps are well behaved.
To illustrate this, consider the simple symmetric matrix,
\[ \begin{pmatrix} 1.000 & 10 \\ 10 & 1.001 \end{pmatrix} \]
Its eigenvalues are evaluated to
\[ \lambda_{+} \approx 11.0005, \qquad \lambda_{-} \approx -8.9995. \]
Although $\lvert a-d \rvert = 0.001$ is extremely small, the true eigenvalue gap
\[ \lvert \lambda_{+} - \lambda_{-} \rvert \approx 2.001 \]
is sufficiently large. Thus, using the diagonal entries $a$ and $d$ as if they were orbital energies would artificially closes the gap. The same phenomenon arises when using AO Fock diagonals.

Newton-Krylov Solver
The question is then whether there is a solver that is oblivious to the choice of gauge.
We demonstrate the use of a Newton-Krylov solver (NK)[3] for solving systems of residual equations. Recall that we seek to solve the system
\[ Z(\theta) = 0 \]
where $Z(\cdot)$ is the residual map in AR basis. The corresponding Newton update is given by
\[ \theta^{(k+1)} = \theta^{(k)} - J^{-1}[Z(\theta^{(k)})] Z(\theta^{(k)}) \]
where "$J$" is the Jacobian of the residual equations evaluated at $\theta^{(k)}$.
A direct inversion is computationally expensive and memory-inefficient. Instead, it is more customary to approximate the Jacobian action. Given a vector $v$, the Jacobian action is approximated by a finite-difference directional derivative
\[ \boxed{ \widetilde{J}(\theta)\, v \;\approx\; \frac{Z(\theta + \delta v) - Z(\theta)}{\delta} } \]
where $\delta$ is a small constant.

[3] C. Yang, J. Brabec, L. Veis, D. B. Williams-Young, and K. Kowalski, Solving coupled cluster equations by the Newton–Krylov method, Front. Chem. 8, 590184 (2020).

Newton-Krylov Solver
Let
\[ w = \theta^{(k+1)} - \theta^{(k)} \]
With this, the Newton equation can be written compactly as
\[ \boxed{ \widetilde{J}[Z(\theta^{(k)})] w = - Z(\theta^{(k)}) } \]
The key idea is to avoid explicitly updating the Jacobian approximation. Instead, the Jacobian-vector product $\widetilde{J}w$ is computed within the GMRES method.

[3] C. Yang, J. Brabec, L. Veis, D. B. Williams-Young, and K. Kowalski, Solving coupled cluster equations by the Newton–Krylov method, Front. Chem. 8, 590184 (2020).
