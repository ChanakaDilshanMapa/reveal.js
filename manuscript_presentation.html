Abstract

Coupled-cluster (CC) theory is accurate but expensive. Local-correlation methods use unitary orbital transformations to exploit locality. The canonical fixed-point solver—based on diagonal energy denominators—can lose contractivity in noncanonical gauges (AO or localized MOs) and diverge. We analyze why this happens and demonstrate that a Newton–Krylov (NK) solver restores convergence across gauges without relying on representation-specific denominators.

Keywords

Coupled-cluster theory; Fixed-point iteration; Orbital gauge; Local correlation; Newton–Krylov

Introduction
  1. CC is size-extensive and systematically improvable; CCD is a core model.
  2. Local approaches (AO, localized MOs, PNOs) reformulate CC via unitary transforms.
  3. Canonical MO basis: diagonal Fock; denominators well motivated by perturbation theory.
  4. Noncanonical gauges: Fock non-diagonal; canonical fixed-point map may not be contractive.
  5. Goal: analyze gauge dependence and provide a robust solver.
CC in an Arbitrary Gauge
CCD correlation energy (MO basis)
\[ E_{\text{corr}} = \sum_{ij}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{occ}}} \sum_{ab}^{\mathcal{N}_{\text{vir}}} w_{ij}^{ab} t_{ij}^{ab} \]
with antisymmetrized integrals \( w_{ij}^{ab} = 2 v_{ij}^{ab} - v_{ij}^{ba} \).
Unitary gauge transform of amplitudes
\[ \theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} = \bar{T}_{\mu i} \bar{T}_{\nu j} \bar{T}_{\lambda a} \bar{T}_{\sigma b} \, t_{ij}^{ab} \]
Energy in AR basis
\[ E_{\text{corr}} = \Pi^{\mu\nu}_{\lambda\sigma} \, \theta_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma} \quad \text{with} \quad \Pi=2\pi-\pi^{\sigma\lambda} \]
Residual Equations (AR)

Residual tensors transform with the orbital rotations from MO to AR. Schematically:

\[ Z_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda\sigma}(\theta) = d + a\,\theta + \theta\,b + \theta\,\pi\,\theta + \Omega(\cdot) = 0. \]
Intermediates \( {}^{v}G, {}^{o}G, j, k, a, b, c, d \) follow projector-based transforms ensuring occ/vir separation.
Fixed-Point Map and Convergence
Canonical MO update
\[ t^{ab}_{ij} = \frac{ v^{ab}_{ij} + \Delta f^{ab}_{ij}(t) + \bar{R}^{ab}_{ij}(t) }{ f^{i}_{i} + f^{j}_{j} - f^{a}_{a} - f^{b}_{b} } \]
AR-basis naive update
\[ \theta^{\lambda\sigma}_{\mu\nu} = \frac{ d + \Delta F(\theta) + R(\theta) }{ {}^{o}F^{\mu}_{\mu} + {}^{o}F^{\nu}_{\nu} - {}^{v}F^{\lambda}_{\lambda} - {}^{v}F^{\sigma}_{\sigma} } \]
  1. Local convergence needs Jacobian spectral radius \(\rho(J) < 1\).
  2. In noncanonical gauges, denominators lose perturbative meaning; contractivity can fail.
Numerical Results and Discussion
  1. MO vs AO gauges for small molecules: MO converges; AO diverges.
  2. Pattern consistent across basis sets; divergence tied to artificial denominators.
  3. Interpretation: AR denominators do not reflect eigenvalue gaps of \(H_0\).

Perturbation-Theory Perspective
Partition
\[ H = H_0 + V, \quad H_0 = \sum_{p,q} F_{pq} a_p^\dagger a_q, \quad V = H - F. \]
MO denominators
\[ \Delta_{ij}^{ab} = \varepsilon_i + \varepsilon_j - \varepsilon_a - \varepsilon_b \]
Diagonal Fock (MO) ties denominators to eigenvalue gaps of \(H_0\).
  1. AO Fock diagonals are not eigenvalues; they can mimic small gaps.
  2. Artificially small \(\Delta\) yields non-contractive updates and divergence.
Newton–Krylov Solver
Newton update
\[ \theta^{(k+1)} = \theta^{(k)} - J^{-1}[Z(\theta^{(k)})] \, Z(\theta^{(k)}) \]
Approximate Jacobian action via finite differences; solve with restarted GMRES.
  1. Treats CC residuals as a general nonlinear system.
  2. Avoids representation-dependent denominators; robust across gauges.
Convergence (MOCC and AOCC)

Empirically converges for both MO and AO gauges across tested molecules; iteration counts decrease as GMRES restart \(m\) increases moderately.

Conclusion
  1. Canonical fixed-point solver is not gauge invariant; stability relies on diagonal Fock.
  2. Noncanonical gauges break perturbative denominators; divergence observed empirically.
  3. Newton–Krylov treats CC as a general nonlinear system; converges across gauges.
  4. Recommendation: use NK (with level-shifting as needed) in localized CC workflows.
